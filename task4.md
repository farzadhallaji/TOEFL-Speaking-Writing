 

## Templates

### **Template for Descriptive Tasks**

**Question Type:** Using points and examples from the lecture, describe [topic].

1. **Introduction:**
   - Begin with a general statement that introduces the topic and mentions the two examples or points to be discussed.
   - Example: "The professor explains two methods/types/functions/ways of [topic], and provides examples to clarify each."
2. **First Point/Example:**
   - State the first point and give the example from the lecture.
   - Example: "Firstly, [method/example] is explained as [definition]. For example, [details about the example]. This demonstrates [conclusion]."
3. **Second Point/Example:**
   - Transition to the second point with a linking phrase.
   - Example: "Secondly, the professor discusses [method/example]. For instance, [details about the second example]. This shows [conclusion]."
4. **Conclusion:**
   - Briefly summarize the two points and their significance.
   - Example: "In conclusion, the professor illustrates [two points] through [examples]. These examples help clarify how [topic is applied/understood]."

------

### **Example Template with Content (Neo 4 - Fossil Usage)**

1. **Introduction:**
   - "The professor explains how fossils are used to study past climates, focusing on the chemical analysis and the study of shellfish fossils."
2. **First Point/Example:**
   - "Firstly, scientists analyze the chemical makeup and mineral proportions in fossils to determine past climates. For instance, shellfish fossils provide valuable data. The professor explains that magnesium levels in these fossils indicate past temperature changes—low magnesium levels suggest cooler climates, while higher levels suggest warmer ones."
3. **Second Point/Example:**
   - "Secondly, shell form and structure reflect environmental conditions. The professor notes that features of shellfish change according to temperature and humidity, providing additional insights into historical climates."
4. **Conclusion:**
   - "In summary, fossils are key tools for understanding past climates through chemical analysis and structural studies, as demonstrated by the examples of shellfish fossils."

### **Template for Comparison Tasks**

**Question Type:** Using points and examples from the lecture, compare two [methods/strategies/examples].

1. **Introduction:**
   - "The professor compares two [methods/strategies/examples] of [topic], explaining how they differ and using examples to highlight their characteristics."
2. **First Comparison Point:**
   - "The first [method/example] is [description]. For example, [example provided by the professor]. This shows that [specific detail]."
3. **Second Comparison Point:**
   - "The second [method/example] is [description], which contrasts with the first because [reason]. For instance, [example provided by the professor]."
4. **Conclusion:**
   - "Overall, the professor demonstrates that [key differences] distinguish these two [methods/examples]. Both are important for [topic]."

------

### **Template for Cause-Effect Tasks**

**Question Type:** Explain how [event/discovery] affects [outcome].

1. **Introduction:**
   - "The professor explains how [event/discovery] led to [outcomes], focusing on two main effects."
2. **First Effect/Example:**
   - "The first effect is [description]. For example, [details from the lecture]. This illustrates [result]."
3. **Second Effect/Example:**
   - "Another significant effect is [description]. The professor provides the example of [example], which shows [result]."
4. **Conclusion:**
   - "In conclusion, [event/discovery] had profound effects, as shown by [examples], leading to [outcomes]."

------

### **Template for Problem-Solution Tasks**

**Question Type:** Explain two [solutions/adaptations/strategies] discussed in the lecture to address [problem].

1. **Introduction:**
   - "The professor discusses [problem] and provides two solutions/adaptations to address it."
2. **First Solution/Adaptation:**
   - "The first solution is [description]. The professor provides the example of [example] to illustrate how this helps."
3. **Second Solution/Adaptation:**
   - "The second solution is [description]. For instance, [example from the lecture], which demonstrates [conclusion]."
4. **Conclusion:**
   - "In summary, the professor highlights [solutions/adaptations], showing their effectiveness in addressing [problem]."

## Common phrases, collocations, and vocabulary

**1. Introduction Phrases**

- "The professor discusses..."
- "The lecture is about..."
- "According to the lecture..."
- "The speaker describes two/three [methods/types/examples] of..."
- "The reading passage focuses on..."
- "The topic of the lecture is..."
- "The professor gives examples of..."
- "The speaker explains how/why..."

------

### **9. Useful Collocations**

- **For Emphasizing:**
  - "plays a vital role"
  - "serves as a key element"
  - "has a major impact"
  - "is highly effective"
  - "makes a significant difference"
- **For Describing Change or Development:**
  - "leads to growth"
  - "results in improvement"
  - "triggers a shift"
  - "enhances the ability to..."
  - "contributes to success"
- **For Evaluating Benefits/Drawbacks:**
  - "offers a clear advantage"
  - "provides a distinct benefit"
  - "poses a challenge to..."
  - "creates complications for..."
  - "results in additional costs"

------

### **10. Synonyms for Common Words**

To avoid repetition:

- **Important**: significant, critical, essential, vital
- **Show**: demonstrate, illustrate, highlight, reveal
- **Explain**: clarify, describe, discuss, outline
- **Cause**: lead to, result in, trigger, bring about
- **Help**: assist, support, aid, facilitate
- **Problem**: challenge, issue, difficulty, obstacle
- **Solution**: resolution, answer, strategy, approach
