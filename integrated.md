## Templates

### **Template 1: General Format**

**Introduction:**
 The reading passage and the lecture discuss [topic]. While the passage presents [number] main points supporting [main claim], the lecture challenges these views, providing alternative perspectives or refutations.

**Body Paragraph 1:**
 Firstly, the reading claims that [point from reading]. However, the lecturer counters this argument by stating that [lecture counterpoint]. [Provide supporting details from the lecture.]

**Body Paragraph 2:**
 Secondly, the reading suggests that [second point from reading]. On the contrary, the lecturer argues that [lecture counterpoint]. [Provide supporting details from the lecture.]

**Body Paragraph 3:**
 Lastly, the passage asserts that [third point from reading]. The lecturer, however, disagrees by stating that [lecture counterpoint]. [Provide supporting details from the lecture.]

**Conclusion:**
 In summary, the lecturer effectively challenges the points made in the reading passage by providing [specific evidence or alternative explanations]. The lecture refutes the reading's arguments, suggesting that [final remark summarizing the contrast].

------

### **Template 2: Comparative Focus**

**Introduction:**
 The passage discusses [topic], suggesting that [summary of main points]. However, the lecturer disagrees with these claims, arguing that [summary of lecturer’s perspective].

**Body Paragraphs:**

- The reading argues that [point from passage]. The lecturer counters this by explaining that [lecture counterpoint]. For example, [support from the lecture].
- According to the passage, [second point from passage]. The lecturer, however, highlights that [lecture counterpoint]. He/She supports this with [specific evidence].
- Lastly, the passage asserts that [third point from passage]. The lecturer refutes this by pointing out that [lecture counterpoint]. This is supported by [details].

**Conclusion:**
 Thus, the lecture casts significant doubt on the reading's arguments, presenting [alternate explanations or evidence].

------

### **Template 3: Rebuttal Style**

**Introduction:**
 The reading passage provides arguments supporting [main claim]. However, the lecturer offers compelling rebuttals to each of these claims, casting doubt on the reading’s conclusions.

**Body Paragraphs:**

- The passage states that [first claim]. The lecturer refutes this by arguing that [lecture rebuttal]. [Expand with details from the lecture.]
- Next, the passage suggests [second claim]. The lecturer, however, contradicts this by explaining that [lecture rebuttal]. [Provide lecture evidence.]
- Finally, the passage posits that [third claim]. In contrast, the lecturer highlights [lecture rebuttal]. [Expand with lecture details.]

**Conclusion:**
 In conclusion, the lecture effectively challenges the validity of the passage by [final assessment of the contrast].

------

### **Template 4: Problem-Solution Comparison**

**Introduction:**
 The reading and lecture address [problem or topic]. While the passage offers several solutions or explanations, the lecturer provides counterarguments that challenge these ideas.

**Body Paragraphs:**

- The reading suggests [solution or theory 1]. However, the lecturer argues that [why this solution is flawed]. For instance, [lecture details].
- Additionally, the passage proposes [solution or theory 2]. In contrast, the lecturer states that [why this idea is impractical or unsupported]. [Include supporting evidence.]
- Lastly, the reading advocates for [solution or theory 3]. The lecturer, however, refutes this by pointing out [counterargument from the lecture]. [Expand with details.]

**Conclusion:**
 Thus, the lecturer casts doubt on the effectiveness or accuracy of the passage’s proposed ideas, emphasizing that [alternative insight from the lecture].

------

### **Template 5: Emphasis on Evidence**

**Introduction:**
 The reading passage and lecture discuss [topic], offering differing perspectives. The reading emphasizes [summary of key claims], while the lecture provides evidence that contradicts or undermines these claims.

**Body Paragraphs:**

- The passage presents evidence suggesting [first claim]. However, the lecturer explains that [why the evidence is insufficient or flawed]. For example, [specific example from the lecture].
- According to the passage, [second claim]. The lecturer counters this by providing [lecture evidence or alternative explanation]. [Expand with details.]
- Finally, the passage highlights [third claim]. On the other hand, the lecturer disputes this with [lecture evidence or counterargument].

**Conclusion:**
 In conclusion, the lecture provides strong evidence that challenges the claims made in the reading, suggesting that [summary of lecture’s perspective].

## Common collocations, phrases, and vocabulary

**Introduction Phrases**

1. The passage discusses ___, while the lecture challenges this by ___.
2. The reading highlights ___, whereas the lecture provides a contrasting view.
3. Both the reading and the lecture examine the topic of ___ but provide differing arguments.
4. While the article argues that ___, the lecturer casts doubt on this claim by ___.
5. The reading and the lecture focus on ___ but offer opposing perspectives.
6. The article explains ___; however, the lecturer contradicts this by asserting ___.
7. This discussion centers around ___, with the reading advocating for ___ and the lecture opposing it.
8. The passage supports the idea that ___, whereas the lecturer disputes this claim, stating that ___.
9. The topic of ___ is debated in both the reading and lecture, with the author proposing ___ and the speaker challenging this view.

------

### **Body Paragraph Starters**

#### **Presenting Points from the Reading**

1. According to the reading, ___.
2. The passage suggests that ___.
3. The reading proposes that ___ as a solution/explanation for ___.
4. One of the main points made in the article is that ___.
5. The article describes ___ as a key factor influencing ___.
6. The passage emphasizes ___, claiming that ___.
7. The reading contends that ___, providing evidence such as ___.
8. The author argues that ___ is critical because ___.

#### **Presenting Counterpoints from the Lecture**

1. The lecturer refutes this by stating ___.
2. The speaker counters this by arguing that ___.
3. The lecturer challenges this argument, asserting that ___.
4. In opposition, the speaker suggests that ___.
5. Contrary to the reading, the lecturer emphasizes ___.
6. This claim is refuted by the lecturer, who argues that ___.
7. The lecture contradicts this point by providing evidence that ___.
8. The lecturer undermines this perspective by pointing out that ___.

------

### **Phrases for Contrasting Arguments**

1. The lecturer disagrees with this idea, arguing that ___.
2. On the contrary, the lecturer asserts that ___.
3. The lecture counters this point by explaining that ___.
4. This argument is undermined by the lecturer, who points out that ___.
5. The speaker casts doubt on this claim by mentioning ___.
6. The lecturer directly opposes this viewpoint, suggesting that ___.
7. This perspective is challenged by the speaker, who highlights that ___.
8. The lecturer raises concerns about ___, arguing instead that ___.
9. The speaker offers a different interpretation, stating that ___.

------

### **Evidence Integration**

1. For instance, the lecturer explains that ___.
2. The article provides evidence such as ___, but the lecturer refutes this by saying ___.
3. The lecturer supports their argument with ___.
4. Additionally, the lecture highlights ___, which contradicts the reading’s claim.
5. To illustrate, the lecturer mentions ___.
6. As evidence, the lecturer points to ___.
7. The lecturer illustrates their point by discussing ___.
8. Supporting their claim, the speaker refers to ___.
9. This idea is further bolstered/undermined by ___, according to the lecture.

------

### **Conclusion Phrases**

1. In conclusion, the lecturer effectively challenges the reading’s claims by ___.
2. The lecture casts significant doubt on the arguments presented in the reading.
3. Ultimately, the speaker’s evidence undermines the claims made in the article.
4. The lecture provides alternative explanations that discredit the points made in the passage.
5. In summary, the reading and lecture present conflicting views on ___, with the lecturer emphasizing ___.
6. The lecturer dismantles the reading’s arguments by offering compelling counterpoints.
7. Overall, the lecture disputes the article’s claims by presenting ___.
8. The speaker’s explanations reveal critical flaws in the passage’s reasoning.
9. This analysis shows that the reading’s perspective is less convincing than the lecturer’s evidence-based arguments.
