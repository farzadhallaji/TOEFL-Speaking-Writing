## **1. Overview of Tasks**

### **Task 2: Summarizing Opinions from Conversations**

- Summarize the opinion of one speaker and their reasons for holding that opinion.
- Include **two main reasons** with examples provided in the conversation.

### **Task 3: Summarizing Academic Content**

- Combine information from a short passage and a lecture to explain a concept or phenomenon.
- Highlight **two key points** from the lecture and how they expand or contrast the reading.

### **Task 4: Explaining Academic Concepts**

- Summarize a lecture explaining a concept, supported by **two examples** or details.
- Focus on **clarity** and **connection** between examples and the concept.

------

## **2. Task 2: Summarizing Conversations**

### **Structure**

1. Introduction:
   - "In the conversation, the [man/woman] expresses [his/her] opinion about [topic]."
   - "The speaker supports/opposes [idea or change] for two main reasons."
2. Reason 1:
   - "First, [reason 1]."
   - "For instance, [specific example from the conversation]."
3. Reason 2:
   - "Second, [reason 2]."
   - "The speaker mentions that [additional example or detail]."
4. Conclusion:
   - "In conclusion, the [man/woman] supports/opposes [topic] because of [reason 1] and [reason 2]."

### **Reusable Phrases**

- "The [man/woman] believes that..."
- "The speaker thinks that this is a good/bad idea because..."
- "One reason is that..."
- "The speaker gives an example of..."
- "Additionally, the [man/woman] mentions that..."
- "This shows that..."
- "In summary, the speaker’s opinion is based on [reason 1] and [reason 2]."

### **Additional Transitions**

- "Furthermore, the speaker explains..."
- "What’s more, the speaker believes that..."
- "On top of that, the [man/woman] thinks that..."
- "To illustrate this point, [he/she] gives the example of..."

## **3. Task 3: Summarizing Academic Content**

### **Structure**

1. Introduction:
   - "The reading passage and lecture discuss [topic]."
   - "While the passage explains [main idea from the text], the professor provides additional examples/details to expand on this concept."
2. Point 1:
   - "First, the passage states that [point from the text]."
   - "The professor elaborates on this by mentioning [specific example]."
3. Point 2:
   - "Second, the reading mentions that [second point]."
   - "However, the professor provides an example of [specific example] to explain this further."
4. Conclusion:
   - "In conclusion, the lecture clarifies and expands on the ideas presented in the reading by providing examples of [topic]."

### **Reusable Phrases**

- "The passage focuses on..."
- "The professor explains that..."
- "According to the lecture, [specific detail]..."
- "The reading suggests that [concept], but the professor adds that..."
- "An example given by the professor is..."
- "This helps to illustrate that..."
- "In summary, the professor’s lecture supports the reading by..."

### **Additional Transitions**

- "Moreover, the professor highlights..."
- "Additionally, the lecture points out..."
- "On the other hand, the professor argues that..."
- "In contrast to the passage, the lecture explains..."
- "This demonstrates how the reading and lecture connect by..."

## **4. Task 4: Explaining Academic Concepts**

### **Structure**

1. Introduction:
   - "The professor explains [concept or topic] and provides two examples to clarify it."
2. Example 1:
   - "First, the professor mentions [example 1] to illustrate [specific point]."
   - "This example shows that [specific explanation]."
3. Example 2:
   - "Second, the lecture describes [example 2], which highlights [another aspect of the concept]."
   - "According to the professor, [specific explanation]."
4. Conclusion:
   - "In conclusion, the professor uses these examples to demonstrate [concept or topic]."

### **Reusable Phrases**

- "The professor discusses [concept] and provides two examples to illustrate it."
- "The first example provided is..."
- "This example highlights that..."
- "The second example is..."
- "According to the professor, this shows that..."
- "In summary, these examples demonstrate that..."
- "By using these examples, the professor clarifies how [concept] works."

### **Additional Transitions**

- "Furthermore, the professor explains..."
- "What’s more, the example demonstrates..."
- "This highlights that..."
- "Additionally, the professor describes how..."
- "This supports the idea that..."

## **6. Full Examples**

### **Task 2 Example**

1. Introduction:
   - "In the conversation, the man disagrees with the university’s decision to limit parking spaces for students. He provides two main reasons for his opinion."
2. Reason 1:
   - "Firstly, he mentions that students often need their cars to commute from home to campus. He explains that many students live far away and public transportation is not always available."
3. Reason 2:
   - "Secondly, the man argues that parking restrictions will force students to waste time searching for available spots, which could make them late for class."
4. Conclusion:
   - "In summary, the man believes the decision is unfair because it makes commuting more difficult and wastes students’ time."

### **Task 3 Example**

1. Introduction:
   - "The reading and lecture discuss the concept of product positioning, which refers to how marketers present products to appeal to target audiences."
2. Point 1:
   - "The reading explains that product positioning focuses on creating a distinct impression in customers’ minds. The professor expands on this by sharing an example of a car company that initially advertised its cars as luxurious but later emphasized fuel efficiency to better meet customers’ needs."
3. Point 2:
   - "Additionally, the reading mentions that positioning does not involve changing the product itself but rather how it is perceived. The professor highlights how this strategy tripled sales after repositioning the car’s image."
4. Conclusion:
   - "In conclusion, the lecture supports the reading by showing how product positioning impacts customer perceptions and sales."

### **Task 4 Example**

1. Introduction:
   - "The professor explains the concept of optimal foraging theory, which suggests that animals maximize energy intake while minimizing effort."
2. Example 1:
   - "The first example is the crow, which searches for clams on the beach. Instead of eating every clam, the crow selects larger clams and drops them from a height to crack them open efficiently."
3. Example 2:
   - "The second example is how this strategy allows the crow to save energy while focusing on high-calorie food sources, demonstrating the balance between effort and reward."
4. Conclusion:
   - "In conclusion, the professor uses these examples to illustrate how animals optimize their foraging strategies for survival."
